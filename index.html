01
Video Game Design · Game UI

Game UI Systems: From Pretty Screens to Interfaces That Actually Work

Speaker: Andres Felipe Pisso Tobar — Lead UX Designer at Teravision Games · Bogota, Colombia 5.0

Summary

This was one of the most practical talks of the whole event. Andres Pisso Tobar, who works as Lead UX Designer at Teravision Games, gave a hands-on workshop about how to design UI systems for video games. The main idea he was trying to explain is that making a good game interface is not just about making it look nice — it's about building a system with clear rules, reusable components and different states that can be used consistently across the whole game.

He started with something that really caught my attention: "A UI can look good and still not work." He explained that the problem is not always having too much stuff on screen. A lot of times the real problem is that nobody defined the rules for what should appear, when it should appear and how it should behave. When there is no system, every new screen has to be built from scratch and that creates a lot of inconsistency and extra work for the team.

During the workshop we built a mini Game UI base in Figma by following 5 steps:

  • 01 Define visual rules — colors, fonts, spacing and text hierarchy.
  • 02 Create reusable components — HUD Stats, Action Prompts and Gameplay Toasts.
  • 03 Design state variants — Normal, Warning, Critical, Reward and Unavailable.
  • 04 Apply the system to a full screen — build a complete HUD using the components.
  • 05 Test with the 3-Second Test — check if the UI makes sense without any explanation.

At the end he made a point that I think applies to all areas of development, not just games: the difference between someone who just makes pretty screens and someone who builds real systems is the discipline to create clear rules first. In a real game studio, having a good design system means you can iterate fast instead of getting stuck fixing the same inconsistency problems over and over.

02
Cybersecurity · Critical Infrastructure · Ericsson

Protection of Critical Digital Infrastructure: Network Governance and Cybersecurity for National Sovereignty

Speaker: Eng. Yanis Stovanel — Customer Security Manager at Ericsson · Bogota, May 8th 2026

Summary

This conference was presented by engineer Yanis Stovanel, who works as Customer Security Manager at Ericsson. Her talk was about a topic that I think is really important and that most people don't pay enough attention to: protecting the critical digital infrastructure of a country. She called this the "Critical Infrastructure Scenario" and explained how cyberattacks on systems like telecommunications networks, power grids, water systems and financial platforms can literally bring a whole country to a stop.

She organized the talk around five key questions that every cybersecurity strategy should answer: Why do they attack? Who is attacking? How do they attack? What are the consequences? And how will we be attacked in the future? She defined vulnerabilities as flaws or weaknesses in systems, software, networks or processes that attackers can use to compromise security.

The numbers she showed were really alarming. According to the KEV Catalog (Known Exploited Vulnerabilities) from CISA, there are about 327,000 documented vulnerabilities and around 1,500 of them are being actively exploited right now. Between 8 and 10% of those affect industrial technologies and active exploitation went up by +20% in 2025. About 40% of all vulnerabilities are classified as High Impact according to the CVSS scoring system.

She also talked about the three main cyber threat groups that specifically target global telecommunications:

  • 01 Salt Typhoon — Active since 2020, this is a state-linked group focused on espionage and stealing information by intercepting network traffic. Mainly operates in South America and Southeast Asia.
  • 02 Liminal Panda — Has attacked telecom organizations using custom tools and deep knowledge of network interconnections. Active in Africa and North America.
  • 03 Tracer Kitten — Focused on intelligence operations against telecom companies. Has been detected in Europe, Africa and the Middle East.

She also showed us something that was pretty scary: the Cyberattack Ecosystem. It's basically a full criminal business chain that goes from Initial Access Brokers (people who sell access to hacked systems) all the way to Money Laundering. In the middle you have things like Malware Operators and Ransomware as a Service (RaaS) — which means criminals can now basically rent malware like a subscription service.

The last part of her talk was about Agentic AI — AI systems that act autonomously, make their own decisions and can carry out complex tasks without human supervision. She explained that this is enabling a new generation of threats like Autonomous Hacking Agents, Polymorphic Malware and Automated Vulnerability Exploitation. Basically, AI is now being used as a weapon.

Her conclusion was clear: cybersecurity can't just be a technical department anymore. It has to become a proactive governance strategy at the highest levels of government and business. Protecting a country's networks is not just a technical challenge — it is a matter of national sovereignty.

TOPICS COVERED

Main Topics

These are the most important technology topics that were discussed during Colombia 5.0.

Artificial Intelligence

AI is the field of computer science that focuses on making machines capable of learning, reasoning and making decisions. In business, AI is used to automate complex tasks, analyze huge amounts of data and improve customer experiences.

Digital Transformation

Digital transformation is the process of using technology to change the way a company operates and delivers value to its customers. It is not just about technology — it also requires changes in culture and business strategy.

Big Data

Big Data refers to very large sets of data that are too complex to be processed with traditional tools. When used correctly, big data analysis helps companies make better decisions based on real evidence instead of guesses.

Cloud Computing

Cloud computing means accessing computing services like servers, storage and databases over the internet instead of having physical hardware. Companies use it to scale faster, reduce costs and keep their services available all the time.

Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks and data from digital attacks. In the modern world, especially after what we heard at Colombia 5.0, cybersecurity is a strategic priority that affects national sovereignty, not just businesses.

Automation

Automation means using technology to perform tasks with minimal human involvement. Tools like Robotic Process Automation (RPA) free up workers from repetitive tasks so they can focus on more creative and valuable work.

Critical Infrastructure

Critical infrastructure includes the essential systems of a country like telecom networks, electricity, water supply and banking. As we learned from the Ericsson conference, protecting these systems from cyberattacks is one of the biggest challenges right now.

Game UI Design

Game UI design is about creating the visual interface that players interact with in a video game. As the workshop showed us, good game UI is not just about looking cool — it requires a proper design system with clear rules, reusable components and multiple states.

1Game UI SystemSistema de UI para JuegosA structured set of visual rules, reusable components and states that define how the interface of a video game looks and behaves consistently across all screens.
2HUD (Heads-Up Display)Pantalla de Informacion en JuegoThe layer of on-screen elements like health bars, ammo counters and minimaps that gives the player real-time information during gameplay.
3Design SystemSistema de DisenoA collection of reusable components guided by clear rules and standards, used to build digital products consistently and efficiently.
4ComponentComponenteA self-contained, reusable UI building block like a button, health bar or notification that keeps the same behavior on every screen it appears.
5Gameplay ToastNotificacion de JuegoA short on-screen notification that pops up temporarily to inform the player about something that just happened, like getting a reward or a warning.
6Action PromptIndicador de AccionA UI element that tells the player what action they can perform at a given moment, for example "Press X to interact" or "Hold B to reload".
7State VariantVariante de EstadoA version of a component that looks different depending on the current situation: Normal, Warning, Critical, Reward or Unavailable.

9FoundationsFundamentos del SistemaThe base rules of a design system including the color palette, typography, spacing scale and text hierarchy that everything else is built on top of.
10Visual HierarchyJerarquia VisualThe way UI elements are organized by importance so the player's eye naturally goes to the most critical information first.
11UX DesignDiseno de Experiencia de UsuarioThe practice of designing digital products by focusing on how users interact with them and whether the experience feels smooth and intuitive.
12PrototypePrototipoAn interactive model of a design that lets you test how it works and how users move through it before actual development begins.
13Visual FeedbackRetroalimentacion VisualThe visual or audio response that the game gives the player after they do something, letting them know whether the action worked or not.
14FigmaFigmaA cloud-based design tool used to create UI designs, interactive prototypes and collaborative design systems in real time.
15Spacing ScaleEscala de EspaciadoA set of predefined spacing values like 4, 16, 24, 32, 48 and 64 px that are used consistently across all components to keep the design balanced and organized.

FINAL REFLECTION

Conclusion and Opinion

My personal thoughts about what I learned at Colombia 5.0 and how it connects to ethics and professional responsibility.

What I Learned at Colombia 5.0

Attending Colombia 5.0 was a really valuable experience for me as a software development student. I think the biggest lesson I took home is that building technology is not just about knowing how to code — it is about understanding what problem you are trying to solve and building solutions that are coherent, useful and responsible. The Game UI workshop showed me that even in design, having a clear system of rules matters more than making things look pretty. The Ericsson conference showed me that the systems we build can have real consequences for the security of an entire country.

Why Technology Matters for Businesses

One thing I realized at this event is that companies that use technology strategically are not just more efficient — they are also more competitive, more resilient and better prepared for the future. Cloud computing, automation and data analytics are not luxuries anymore. They are tools that any company needs to survive and grow in today's market. Colombia 5.0 made it clear that Colombia has the talent to lead this transformation, but we need to invest in education, infrastructure and security to make it happen.

Artificial Intelligence and Its Benefits

A lot of people are afraid of AI because they think it will take their jobs. But what I understood from the event is that AI is more of a tool than a replacement. It can help doctors make better diagnoses, help farmers manage their crops, help teachers personalize education and help companies reduce waste. That said, what the Ericsson conference also showed is that AI can be used for bad things too, like automating cyberattacks. That is why how we develop and regulate AI matters so much.

Responsible Use of Data

Data is probably the most valuable resource in the digital economy right now. But with that value comes a big responsibility. Companies and governments have to use data in a way that respects people's privacy, gets their informed consent and follows regulations like Colombia's Law 1581. I think as a future developer, one of the most important things I can do is make sure the systems I build protect user data instead of exposing it.

Ethics in Business Technology

Colombia 5.0 made me think a lot about the ethical side of technology. AI algorithms can reinforce existing inequalities if they are trained on biased data. Systems can be designed to manipulate people instead of helping them. Critical infrastructure can be left unprotected because security was treated as an afterthought. I believe that every technology decision has an ethical dimension, and as future professionals we have to take that seriously. Ethics should not be something we add at the end — it has to be part of the design process from the beginning.

Social Impact of Automation

Automation creates a real tension that I think is hard to ignore. On one hand it makes companies more efficient and competitive. On the other hand it displaces workers who do repetitive jobs and do not always have the resources to learn new skills. I think the responsible approach is to invest in retraining programs, design systems that are accessible to everyone and make sure the benefits of automation are shared broadly across society — not just by the companies that implement it.

Professional Responsibility in Software Development

As a software development student, this event made me think about what it really means to be a responsible developer. It is not just about writing code that works — it is about writing code that is clean, documented, secure and ethical. We are building the systems that people and institutions rely on every day. When those systems fail or are misused, there are real consequences for real people. Colombia 5.0 reminded me that the best thing we can do as future developers is take that responsibility seriously from day one.
